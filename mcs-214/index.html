UNIT 1: THE PROCESS OF COMMUNICATION

1.1 What is Communication?

Definition: Exchange of thoughts or making known information/feelings by speech, writing, or other means.[1]

Communication involves:

  • Words, facial expressions, tone, body language
  • Use of all senses
  • More than just messaging‚Äîcreates value through shared perspectives

1.2 The Communication Process

Communication Model:[1]

SENDER (Encoder) ‚Üí MESSAGE (Text) ‚Üí RECEIVER (Decoder)

Elements of Communication:[1]

  1. Addresser - The sender
  2. Addressee - The receiver
  3. Topic - Contents of the message
  4. Channel - Medium (letter, telephone, email, etc.)
  5. Code - Language used (English, Hindi, etc.)
  6. Message Form - Grammar and lexical choices
  7. Setting - Social and physical context

Role of the Decoder:[1]

  • Active role - not passive
  • Language has potential for meaning, decoder creates actual meaning
  • Meaning depends on: background knowledge, context, relationship, physical/social setting

Example: "Mr. Gupta is not coming"[1]

  • Can mean: statement, warning, instruction (depending on context)
  • Secretary interprets: meeting cancelled, arrangements stopped

1.3 Macro Functions of Communication[1]

  1. Emotive - Express emotions ("Oh no!")
  2. Directive - Affect behavior ("Close the door, please")
  3. Phatic - Open/check channel ("Hello, is it Thomas Cook?")
  4. Poetic - Aesthetic function of language
  5. Referential - Carry information
  6. Metalinguistic - Focus on code itself
  7. Contextual - Create specific context ("Right! Let's start")

1.4 Barriers to Communication[1]

Main Barriers:

a) Code - Different languages between sender/receiver

b) Vocabulary - Technical terms not understood ("bear hammering" in stock market)

c) Concept - Technical concepts unclear ("black hole")

d) Background Knowledge - Cultural references ("Victorian mansion")

e) Pronunciation/Intonation - Accent/stress differences

f) Culture - Culture-specific communication causing misunderstanding

g) Physical Environment - Noise, distance

h) Affective Factors - Anxiety, fear, attitude, motivation, beliefs, lack of trust, time pressure, rivalries

1.5 Types of Communication

By Expression:[1]

1. Written Communication

  • Forms: Letters, memos, telegrams, fax, newsletters, brochures, bulletins, reports, manuals
  • Most popular in business
  • Advantages: Permanent record, complete, precise, uniform transmission
  • Disadvantages: Time-consuming, expensive, rigid, difficult to maintain secrecy

2. Oral Communication

  • Forms: Face-to-face, telephone, lectures, meetings, discussions
  • Advantages: Quicker, economical, immediate feedback, doubts cleared instantly
  • Disadvantages: Less reliable, no future record, affected by dialects/accent, less organized, unsuitable for lengthy distant messages

3. Gestural Communication

  • Body language, actions, gestures
  • Used when verbal communication impossible (noisy factory)

By Flow:[1]

1. Downward Communication

  • Superior to subordinate
  • Orders, instructions, decisions

2. Upward Communication

  • Subordinate to superior
  • Reports, suggestions, feedback

3. Horizontal/Lateral Communication

  • Between same-level officers
  • Exchange of ideas, information, clarifications

By Relationship:[1]

1. Formal Communication

  • Through chain of command
  • Official messages in organizational structure
  • Planned by management
  • Generally in writing

2. Informal Communication

  • Not through official channels
  • Spontaneous, usually oral
  • May carry incomplete/incorrect information

1.6 Conversation Characteristics[1]

Grice's Maxims (Cooperative Principle):

  1. Maxim of Quantity - Just as informative as required
  2. Maxim of Quality - Make contribution true
  3. Maxim of Relation - Make contribution relevant
  4. Maxim of Manner - Avoid obscurity/ambiguity, be brief and orderly

Adjacency Pairs:[1]

Two successive utterances where second is expected follow-up to first

Examples:

  • Greeting-Greeting: "Hi" / "Hi"
  • Question-Answer: "Do you have apples?" / "Yes, here they are"
  • Compliment-Acceptance: "Nice hat" / "Thanks"
  • Summons-Answer: "John!" / "Yes, Mom?"

Turn-Taking Rules:

  • When speaker produces first pair part, should stop talking
  • Next speaker produces second pair part
  • Provides structure to conversation

1.7 Conversation vs Other Speech Events[1]

Conversation: Reciprocal, overt interaction, immediate feedback, adjacency pairs, turn-taking

Lecture: Non-reciprocal, covert interaction, no immediate feedback, speaker enacts both roles

Other Events: Discussions, meetings, interviews, debates - each has specific rules for beginnings, endings, participants, and speaking patterns

  • "Hello, [Company name], can I help you?"
  • "[Name] speaking"
  • "How may I help you?"

  • "Can I speak to [name]?"
  • "Could you put me through to [name]?"
  • "Is [name] available?"
  • "Can you get me extension [number]?"

Connecting:

  • "Hold on a minute"
  • "I'll put you through"
  • "Just a moment, please"
  • "One moment, I'll transfer you"

When Person Unavailable:

  • "I'm afraid he's/she's out at the moment"
  • "He's/She's not available right now"
  • "He's/She's in a meeting"
  • "Can I take a message?"

Leaving a Message:

  • "Could you ask him/her to call me?"
  • "Please tell him/her that..."
  • "My number is..."
  • "It's urgent"

Clarifying:

  • "Could you repeat that, please?"
  • "Could you spell that?"
  • "Let me confirm..."

DO: ‚úÖ Answer promptly (within 3 rings) ‚úÖ Identify yourself clearly ‚úÖ Speak clearly and slowly ‚úÖ Be polite and professional ‚úÖ Take accurate messages ‚úÖ Return calls promptly

UNIT 3: JOB APPLICATIONS AND INTERVIEWS

3.1 Job Application Letter

Structure:

  1. Your Address (top right)
  2. Date
  3. Employer's Address (left)
  4. Salutation (Dear Sir/Madam/Mr./Ms.)
  5. Opening Paragraph - State purpose, source of information
  6. Body Paragraphs - Qualifications, experience, why suitable
  7. Closing Paragraph - Request interview, availability
  8. Complimentary Close (Yours sincerely/faithfully)

Tips:

  • Be concise and specific
  • Highlight relevant qualifications
  • Show enthusiasm
  • Proofread carefully
  • Tailor to specific job

Essential Sections:

1. Personal Information

  • Name, contact details, email

2. Career Objective/Profile

  • Brief statement of goals

3. Education

  • Reverse chronological order
  • Degrees, institutions, years, grades

4. Work Experience

  • Reverse chronological order
  • Job title, company, dates, responsibilities

5. Skills

  • Technical, language, computer skills

6. Achievements/Awards

7. Hobbies/Interests

8. References

CV Writing Tips:

  • Keep to 1-2 pages
  • Use bullet points
  • Be truthful
  • Update regularly
  • Use action verbs

3.3 Interview Skills

Types of Interviews:[1]

1. Traditional Interview

  • Broad questions: "Why do you want this job?"
  • Tests communication confidence
  • Focus on: strengths, weaknesses, experience

2. Behavioral Interview

  • Based on past performance
  • Questions: "Tell me about a time when..."
  • Probe specific past behaviors

Answering Behavioral Questions (4-Part Outline):[1]

  1. Describe situation truthfully
  2. Discuss actions you took
  3. Relate outcomes
  4. Specify what you learned

Interview Preparation:

Before:

  • Research the company
  • Prepare answers to common questions
  • Prepare questions to ask
  • Dress appropriately
  • Arrive early

During:

  • Greet confidently
  • Maintain eye contact
  • Listen carefully
  • Answer clearly and concisely
  • Be honest
  • Show enthusiasm
  • Ask relevant questions

After:

  • Thank the interviewer
  • Send thank-you email
  • Follow up appropriately

Common Interview Questions:

  1. Tell me about yourself
  2. Why do you want this job?
  3. What are your strengths/weaknesses?
  4. Where do you see yourself in 5 years?
  5. Why should we hire you?

UNIT 4: GROUP DISCUSSIONS

4.1 What is GD?[1]

Definition: Test of interpersonal communication skills to evaluate effectiveness in group activity

Assesses:

  • Capacity to interact constructively
  • Make meaningful contributions
  • Structure arguments logically
  • Communicate ideas effectively

Requirements for Success:

  1. Good knowledge base
  2. Ability to structure arguments logically
  3. Skill to communicate effectively

4.2 Types of GD[1]

1. Topic-Based

  • Given a topic to discuss
  • Examples: "Should India adopt two-child policy?", "Coalition politics"

2. Case Study-Based

  • Given case to read and analyze
  • Requires more preparation time

4.3 What GD Assesses[1]

  1. Knowledge Base - General awareness, current affairs
  2. Communication Skills - Clarity, language, listening, persuasion
  3. Interpersonal Skills - Teamwork, respect, conflict resolution
  4. Leadership Qualities - Initiative, guidance, time management
  5. Logical Thinking - Analytical ability, problem-solving
  6. Personality Traits - Confidence, assertiveness, maturity

4.4 Your Objective in GD[1]

"To be SEEN to have contributed MEANINGFULLY in an ATTEMPT to achieve the right consensus"

Three Key Words:

1. SEEN[1]

  • Must be seen by evaluator
  • Must be heard by group
  • Get adequate airtime

2. MEANINGFULLY[1]

  • Substantial and relevant contributions
  • Shows knowledge, logical structure, good communication
  • Rule 1: In chaos - person who restores order is appreciated
  • Rule 2: When floundering - person who gives fresh direction gets credit

3. ATTEMPT[1]

  • Must attempt to build consensus
  • Shows maturity and team spirit

4.5 Do's of GD[1]

‚úÖ Ensure adequate airtime ‚úÖ Build good knowledge base (read newspapers, watch news) ‚úÖ Speak confidently and clearly ‚úÖ Listen actively ‚úÖ Be respectful ‚úÖ Support arguments with facts/examples ‚úÖ Help build consensus ‚úÖ Show leadership when appropriate ‚úÖ Encourage quieter members

4.6 Don'ts of GD[1]

‚ùå Don't be aggressive ‚ùå Don't let high performers overshadow low performers ‚ùå Don't drift from main topic ‚ùå Don't be verbose and wordy ‚ùå Don't ask moderators about your performance ‚ùå Don't monopolize discussion ‚ùå Don't speak without thinking ‚ùå Don't be passive/silent ‚ùå Don't use offensive language ‚ùå Don't look at evaluator (speak to group!)

4.7 Language for GD[1]

Expressing Opinion:

  • "In my opinion..."
  • "I believe that..."
  • "From my perspective..."
  • "It seems to me..."

Agreeing:

  • "I agree with you"
  • "That's a valid point"
  • "I'm of the same opinion"
  • "You've made an excellent point"

Disagreeing Politely:

  • "I'm afraid I don't quite agree..."
  • "I see your point, but..."
  • "I have a slightly different view..."
  • "That's one way of looking at it, but..."

Interrupting Politely:

  • "May I add something here?"
  • "If I may interrupt..."
  • "Can I just add a point?"

Building on Ideas:

  • "Building on what [name] said..."
  • "To add to [name]'s point..."

Bringing Back on Track:

  • "I think we're moving away from the main topic..."
  • "Let's get back to the main issue..."

4.8 Opening Speaker Strategy[1]

HIGH-RISK, HIGH-RETURN Strategy

Advantages:

  • Maximum uninterrupted airtime
  • Set tone and direction
  • Strong first impression

Disadvantages:

  • If nonsense, maximum attention to shortcomings
  • Marked as speaking without thinking

Rule: Speak first ONLY if you have something sensible to say. Otherwise, keep quiet!

4.9 Connectives in GD[1]

Adding: Moreover, Furthermore, In addition, Besides

Contrasting: However, Nevertheless, On the other hand, Although

Examples: For instance, For example, Such as

Cause/Effect: Therefore, Thus, Consequently, As a result

Sequencing: First, Second, Finally, Lastly

Emphasizing: Indeed, In fact, Actually, Certainly

UNIT 5: MANAGING ORGANISATIONAL STRUCTURE

5.1 Organizational Structure

Hierarchy Levels:

1. Top Management

  • Sets overall direction
  • Makes strategic decisions
  • Represents organization

2. Middle Management

  • Implements strategies
  • Coordinates departments
  • Reports to top management

3. Lower Management/Supervisors

  • Oversees day-to-day operations
  • Manages frontline employees
  • Ensures task completion

4. Employees

  • Execute assigned tasks
  • Report to supervisors
  • Contribute to organizational goals

5.2 Communication Flow[1]

1. Downward Communication

  • From superiors to subordinates
  • Orders, instructions, policies, decisions
  • Example: Manager ‚Üí Employee

2. Upward Communication

  • From subordinates to superiors
  • Reports, suggestions, feedback, complaints
  • Example: Employee ‚Üí Supervisor

3. Horizontal/Lateral Communication

  • Between peers at same level
  • Coordination, information sharing
  • Example: Between department managers

5.3 Workplace Behavior

Professional Conduct:

1. Punctuality

  • Arrive on time
  • Respect deadlines
  • Manage time effectively

2. Professional Appearance

  • Dress appropriately
  • Maintain hygiene
  • Present professionally

3. Communication

  • Speak clearly and respectfully
  • Listen actively
  • Respond appropriately
  • Use proper channels

  • Work well with others
  • Share information

5. Work Ethics

  • Complete tasks responsibly
  • Take ownership
  • Maintain confidentiality
  • Show initiative

6. Conflict Resolution

  • Stay calm under pressure

5.4 Leadership Qualities

Essential Traits:

1. Vision - See big picture, set clear goals

2. Communication - Express clearly, listen, give feedback

3. Integrity - Honest, ethical, lead by example

4. Decision-Making - Analyze objectively, decide timely, take responsibility

5. Motivation - Inspire team, recognize achievements

6. Delegation - Assign appropriately, trust team, provide support

7. Adaptability - Flexible to change, learn from mistakes

Leadership Styles:

1. Autocratic

  • Leader makes decisions independently
  • Little input from team
  • Best for: Emergencies, quick decisions

2. Democratic

  • Team participates in decisions
  • Leader values everyone's input
  • Best for: Creative projects, team building

3. Laissez-faire

  • Minimal direction from leader
  • Team has freedom
  • Best for: Highly skilled, self-motivated teams

4. Transformational

  • Inspires and motivates change
  • Focuses on vision/innovation
  • Best for: Organizations needing transformation

5. Situational

  • Adapts style to situation
  • Flexible approach
  • Best for: Dynamic, changing environments

  • Proofread carefully

UNIT 6: MEETINGS

6.1 Types of Meetings[1]

Group Meetings:

  • More formal than one-to-one meetings
  • More structured and planned
  • Last longer
  • Involve large number of people
  • Must be chaired by somebody
  • Follow agenda and minutes procedure

One-to-One Meetings:

  • Less formal
  • Not chance conversations - have specific purpose
  • Require planning for desired outcomes
  • Language is less formal

6.2 Conducting a Meeting[1]

Before the Meeting:

‚úÖ Call meeting only when essential - be clear about purpose ‚úÖ Circulate memo several days in advance with:

  • Time, place, venue
  • Objectives
  • Issues to be discussed
  • List of participants
  • Preparation expected

During the Meeting:

‚úÖ Keep meetings short (not more than 1 hour) ‚úÖ Optimal size: 6 participants for working meeting ‚úÖ Draw out quieter members - create relaxed atmosphere ‚úÖ Ensure decisions are taken

After the Meeting:

‚úÖ Circulate minutes with:

  • Decisions taken
  • Person delegated to each task
  • Deadlines for completion ‚úÖ Circulate minutes again before next meeting

6.3 Attending a Meeting[1]

Preparation:

  • Prepare key points in note form
  • Don't memorize or read like sermon
  • Be ready to say "I don't know" if you can't answer

During Meeting:

  • Use natural gestures, eye contact, body language
  • Phrase criticisms positively
  • Offer solutions rather than complain

6.4 Language for Meetings[1]

Opening a Meeting (4-Step Method):

Step 1: Open

  • "Thank you for coming. It's ten o'clock. Let's start."
  • "OK, shall we begin?"

Step 2: Explain Objectives

  • "We're here today to discuss..."
  • "What we want to do today is reach a decision..."

Step 3: Present Agenda

  • "On the agenda you'll see there are three items..."
  • "Have you all seen a copy of the...?"

Step 4: Get Initial Contributions

  • "I'd like to ask Sunidhi to..."

Middle of Meeting:

Stating Opinion:

  • "It seems to me..."
  • "In my view..."
  • "I tend to think..."

Asking for Opinion:

  • "Could we hear from...?"
  • "What do you think about...?"
  • "What's your view?"

Interrupting:

  • "Excuse me, may I ask for clarification..."
  • "If I may interrupt..."

Handling Interruptions:

  • "Yes, go ahead..."
  • "Sorry, please let him finish..."

Moving Discussion On:

  • "Can we go on to think about..."
  • "Let's move on to the next point..."

Expressing Agreement/Disagreement:

STRONG Agreement:

  • "I'm in complete agreement"
  • "I couldn't agree more"
  • "Exactly"
  • "You're right there"

NEUTRAL Agreement:

  • "I agree"
  • "I quite agree"
  • "I think you're right"
  • "That's true..."

STRONG Disagreement:

  • "I disagree completely"
  • "That's out of the question"
  • "That's ridiculous"

NEUTRAL Disagreement:

  • "I don't agree"
  • "That's not how I see it"
  • "I wouldn't say that at all"

Closing Meeting:

  • "I think we've covered everything..."
  • "So we've decided..."
  • "I think we can close the meeting"

6.5 Arranging One-to-One Meetings[1]

Useful Expressions:

  • "How about meeting at...?"
  • "That should be fine. What time?"
  • "What about after...?"
  • "I suggest..."
  • "Let's say 7 pm"
  • "Good idea. See you then"

6.6 Meeting Documents[1]

Agenda:

List of topics/items to be discussed in order

Minutes:

  • Written record of meeting
  • Include: decisions taken, action items, deadlines, responsible persons
  • Circulated to all participants after meeting

Memo:

  • Internal communication
  • Used to invite participants, share information
  • Contains: To, From, Date, Subject

6.7 Reporting Verbs[1]

When writing minutes, use variety of reporting verbs:

  • Suggested - "CA suggested raising the budget"
  • Promised - "MB promised to find out more"
  • Mentioned - "RJ mentioned the possibility of extra funds"
  • Wondered - "DM wondered if there would be extra funding"
  • Agreed - "Everyone agreed to the proposal"
  • Reported - "The Sales Manager reported on the results"
  • Recommended - "Five strategies were recommended"
  • Explained - "SB explained the reason for the delay"

6.8 Criteria for Successful Meetings[1]

Three Key Areas:

1. Content:

  • Focus on key issues
  • Avoid routine meetings that waste time

2. Design:

  • Appropriate meeting structure
  • Clear decision-making process
  • Proper agenda, time frames

3. Process:

  • Ensure individual and group needs met
  • People participating and included
  • Deal with dysfunctional behaviors openly
  • Positive energy in group
  • Commitment to task

Before Presentation:

‚úÖ Know your subject well ‚úÖ Know your audience (size, knowledge level) ‚úÖ Be clear about purpose (inform or persuade) ‚úÖ Check room, seating, acoustics, equipment ‚úÖ Prepare outline well in advance ‚úÖ Rehearse to time it and familiarize with content ‚úÖ Memorize introduction for confident start ‚úÖ Keep sentences short and simple

7.2 Delivery Tips[1]

‚úÖ Be enthusiastic (but not loud) ‚úÖ Vary tone and pitch - proper modulation ‚úÖ Use deliberate pauses to emphasize ‚úÖ Involve audience and respond to reactions ‚úÖ Adapt if your plan isn't working ‚úÖ Treat audience as equals ‚úÖ Pay attention to body language: eye contact, gestures, posture, movement

UNIT 8: PRESENTATION SKILLS-II

8.1 Common Presentation Structures[1]

1. Topical

  • Several ideas presented
  • One naturally precedes the other
  • Useful for informative presentations

2. Chronological

  • Uses time sequence as framework
  • Good for providing background
  • Useful for informative and persuasive presentations

3. Classification

  • Puts things into categories
  • Example: Finance, Marketing, Production

4. Problem-Solution

  • Part 1: Outlines problem
  • Part 2: Presents solution
  • Mostly for persuasive presentations

5. Cause-Effect

  • Part 1: Describes cause of problem
  • Part 2: Describes its effect
  • Used for persuasive speeches

  • Easier to understand
  • Reduce amount of talking

Guidelines:

‚úÖ Ensure visuals are relevant, appropriate, clear ‚úÖ Don't overload with information ‚úÖ Allow time for audience to absorb ‚úÖ Use to reinforce and clarify, not overwhelm ‚úÖ Introduce and highlight briefly and clearly

8.3 Language for Visual Aids[1]

  • "I'd like to show you a pie chart..."
  • "Have a look at this table..."

Verbs:

  • Increase/decrease

  • Grow/decline
  • Improve/deteriorate

Adverbs (Degree):

  • Dramatically

  • Considerably

8.4 Ending the Presentation[1]

Summary:

  • Restates main points
  • Emphasizes what audience must remember
  • Must be brief
  • No new information

Conclusion:

  • States logical consequences of what was said
  • Contains recommendations
  • Must also be brief

Example Structure:

Summary: "I'd like to end by emphasizing the main points:

  • Contrary to expectations, exports have not risen
  • This is mainly due to inability to handle foreign buyers
  • Marketing division has faced cultural barriers"

Conclusion (Recommendations): "So what do we conclude?

  • We must have specific training in communication skills
  • Give exposure to culture of foreign buyers
  • Send them to International Trade Fairs
  • Encourage overseas visits"

8.5 Handling Questions[1]

Guidelines:

‚úÖ Anticipate questions and prepare responses ‚úÖ Rehearse answers to difficult questions ‚úÖ Listen carefully - don't interrupt ‚úÖ Make sure you understand - paraphrase if necessary ‚úÖ Repeat question if needed ‚úÖ Give yourself time to think ‚úÖ Be honest - say so if you can't answer ‚úÖ Keep control - don't allow chaos ‚úÖ Entertain only relevant questions ‚úÖ Watch the time ‚úÖ Be polite ‚úÖ Thank audience at end

Useful Phrases:

  • "If I understood you correctly..."
  • "I'm afraid I don't see the connection"
  • "To be honest, I don't have that information"
  • "Can we ensure we speak one at a time?"
  • "I'm afraid that's all we have time for"
  • "Thank you for being such enthusiastic participants"

8.6 Overcoming Podium Panic[1]

UNIT 11: COPYRIGHT AND PLAGIARISM

11.1 Copyright

Definition:

Legal right that grants creator exclusive rights to use, reproduce, and distribute their original work

What's Protected:

  • Literary works - books, articles, reports
  • Musical works - songs, compositions
  • Artistic works - paintings, photographs, sculptures
  • Dramatic works - plays, films
  • Software - computer programs
  • Databases

Copyright Owner Can:

  • Reproduce the work
  • Distribute copies
  • Display/perform publicly
  • Create derivative works
  • License rights to others

Duration:

Generally lifetime of author + 60 years (varies by country)

11.2 Fair Use

Permitted Uses:

‚úÖ Education - teaching, research ‚úÖ Criticism and review ‚úÖ News reporting ‚úÖ Parody ‚úÖ Limited quotation with proper citation

Factors Considered:

  1. Purpose - commercial vs. educational
  2. Nature of copyrighted work
  3. Amount used - small portion vs. substantial
  4. Effect on market - does it harm original's value?

11.3 Plagiarism

Definition:

Using someone else's work/ideas without giving proper credit - presenting it as your own

Types of Plagiarism:

1. Direct Plagiarism

  • Copy-pasting without citation
  • Word-for-word copying
  • Most serious form

2. Self-Plagiarism

  • Reusing your own previous work
  • Without disclosure
  • Also unethical

3. Mosaic Plagiarism

  • Mixing copied phrases with own words
  • Without quotation marks
  • Paraphrasing without citation

4. Accidental Plagiarism

  • Forgetting to cite sources
  • Misquoting
  • Unintentional paraphrasing

11.4 How to Avoid Plagiarism

Best Practices:

‚úÖ Always cite sources - give credit to original authors ‚úÖ Use quotation marks for direct quotes ‚úÖ Paraphrase properly - rewrite in your own words AND cite ‚úÖ Keep track of sources while researching ‚úÖ Use citation style - APA, MLA, Chicago, etc. ‚úÖ Include bibliography/references ‚úÖ Use plagiarism detection tools before submission

11.5 Citation Basics

When to Cite:

  • Direct quotes
  • Paraphrased ideas
  • Statistics and data
  • Images, charts, graphs
  • Theories and concepts
  • Specific facts (not common knowledge)

What Doesn't Need Citation:

  • Common knowledge
  • Your own original ideas
  • Your own research/experiments
  • Well-known facts

Common Citation Styles:

APA (American Psychological Association):

  • Used in: Psychology, Education, Social Sciences
  • Format: Author-Date system

MLA (Modern Language Association):

  • Used in: Humanities, Literature
  • Format: Author-Page system

Chicago:

  • Used in: History, Arts
  • Format: Notes and Bibliography

11.6 Intellectual Property

Types:

1. Patents

  • Protect inventions
  • Duration: 20 years

2. Trademarks

  • Protect brand names, logos
  • Can be renewed indefinitely

3. Trade Secrets

  • Confidential business information
  • Formulas, practices, designs

4. Copyright

  • As discussed above

11.7 Consequences of Plagiarism

Academic:

  • Failed assignment/course
  • Expulsion from institution
  • Damaged reputation
  • Loss of degree

Professional:

  • Job termination
  • Legal action
  • Damaged credibility
  • Loss of professional license
  • Financial penalties

Legal:

  • Copyright infringement lawsuits
  • Monetary damages
  • Criminal charges (in severe cases)
